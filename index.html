About Our Project

Abstract

The HoloRepository Portable 2021 is a portable, Windows-based, medical 3D model viewer application. It aims to provide a convenient way for clinicians and educators to view, manipulate and store information about medical 3D models on their personal laptop or desktop.

Project Background

Our clients are:

  • Dr Dean Mohemedally (UCL)
  • Sheena Visram (GOSH/UCL)
  • Costas Stylianou (Intel)

The HoloRepository is an open source, FHIR enabled research project that uses pre-trained neural networks to convert from 2D CT and MRI DICOM scans to 3D models in the form of GLB and GLTF files. Our contribution to this project will put less of an emphasis on the use of these cutting edge technologies, but more on how their results (ie, the generated 3D organ models) could be best made use of.

    

Project Goals

     

Our project aims to provide a convenient way for clinicians and educators to view, manipulate and store information about 3D medical models. The HoloRepository Portable 2021 serves as a proof of concept for an application that could be used in both a clinical and educational setting.

    

Requirements Gathering

The first stage of our requirements gathering involved meetings and interviews with our clients. These meetings enabled us to gain a better understanding of what we needed to build, and in doing so allowed us to establish an initial set of must-have requirements.
To further our requirements gathering we carried out semi-structured interviews with pseudo-users. We chose to carry out semi-structured interviews to ensure that we would always get answers to the questions we had prepared, but also allowed us to probe deeper by asking additional questions if the interviewee said anything insightful that we had not considered initially. This ensured that the requirements we gathered were a truer reflection of what a user would look for in a medical model viewer application.
Over the course of the development of our project, we met with our clients on a weekly basis. Our requirements have been expanded upon and subject to change over the course of development based on the requests of our clients, but by meeting and demonstrating our progress frequently, we've ensured that our application is indeed delivering what our clients had in mind.

After a scan of a patient revealed a treatable problem with the patient, he printed out an image of the scan. Drouse felt limited in his explanation by his inability to interact with the scan. If he could’ve zoomed in on and annotated the problem area, he’s sure there would’ve been zero conflict. ​

He often tried to share his enthusiasm with his students, but some of them weren't interested and some didn't even understand what he was talking about. Repeating the explanation over and over again made him physically and mentally tired, but he did not give up. He believes a human organ model rendering program could help a lot.

Research

Related technology review - HoloRepository 2020

The HoloRepository 2020 consists of 3 major component parts[1]:
1. HoloRepository 2020 for Azure and HoloLens 2. Works with the newly released HoloLens 2 and facilitates the cloud storage of 3D models.
2. HoloRepository 2020 for NUCs. Mainly built for clinical research.
3. HoloRepository 2020 Viewer. This is a tool designed for clinicians to teach with that uses the Holopipelines to convert from CT and MRI DICOM scans to 3D GLB models.[2]

The HoloRepository 2020 Viewer serves as a very useful point of comparison for our application, as it is a 3D model viewer application built to run locally. Seeing the HoloRepository 2020 viewer in operation [9] has been extremely useful, particularly in the early stages of sketching and prototyping when trying to consider what users would require from a 3D medical model viewer application. Some of the features in particular stuck out to us as extremely useful:

  • The ability to change the colour of a loaded model
  • The ability to change the opacity of a loaded model
  • The ability to cycle through the segments of the loaded model

Unity and why it's a good choice for our application

Unity is a cross-platform game engine initially released for Mac OS X in June 2005.[6] It is a good choice of technology for our purposes because, as a Game Engine, it must offer fast real time rendering[10]. This is hugely important for us, as it will enable us to offer a performant experience to users even when viewing the extremely complex meshes that can be generated from patient scans.

Furthermore, Unity also keeps a number of the complex ideas underpinning computer graphics abstract and easily accessible to the programmer, which will be useful for us as we are new to the field of computer graphics. For instance, Unity provides two ways of accessing and manipulating the properties of a mesh: using the simple API or using the advanced API.[10]

Another reason that Unity is a good choice is that the main language that it supports is C#. C# is an object-oriented, statically typed language[8] for which there exists a wealth of well maintained tutorials and documentation online. Being able to work using such a well established, and compared to some languages such as C++, easy to learn language will help development as straightforward as possible. The point about there being a number of tutorials available for C# holds true for Unity specifically as well, which will, again, be very beneficial as we are all approaching this project with very little experience in this field.

GLTFUtility v.s. UnityGLTF

As Unity does not natively support GLB and GLTF files, an open-source library is needed. As loading GLB and GLTF files is such an important part of our project, it was important that we chose the right library. The two main ones we came across in our research were:
1. GLTFUtility:
This library is developed by Siccity, who both updates the library himself and manages pull requests from other developers frequently.[3] The library being active is a good sign that if bugs are discovered they will be found. The creator has commented that "GLTFUtility is built with a primary focus on simplicity, does not use external libraries, and can easily navigate to potential contributors. It's plug and play.[5]" This description is very apt.
2. UnityGLTF:
This library is developed by KhronosGroup, the creators of the GLTF file format. UnityGLTF boasts a greater number of features than GLTFUtility, but at the expense of a much more complicated set-up process. However, because of its complexity, UnityGLTF becomes a bit messy and many set-ups need to be prepared while using.[5]Additionaly, both pull requests and commits to the main branch of the GitHub repository are far less frequent than that of GLTFUtility.[4]

As we do not require any of the more complex functionalites offered by UnityGLTF, we have chosen to use GLTFUtility due to its very simple set-up process and easy to use API for importing GLB and GLTF files into Unity at runtime.

The main function of our application is to render 3D model, which means the model is the most important part in our GUI. Therefore, we gave the 3D model the middle position and placed the other interactive buttons at the edge of the screen.

According to the relevant knowledge of HCI we learned, we selected the advantages from the above schemes and started to create and design UI in Figma.

Final Version of UI

In order to solve these problems, we finally turned to a design approach that has recently emerged -- the Neumorphism. Neumorphism is a graphical style that works by giving realism to the elements of a user interface. Its advantage is that the screen is extremely simple, easy to highlight the main body, and will give the user the feeling of  "this software is very easy to use".

Design Patterns

Singleton pattern

A number of the classes in our codebase are dependent on the properties of the loaded model (such as its centre and radius). The ModelHandler class holds a reference to the Organ object, which is an abstract class that represents the loaded model. The ModelHandler class is responsible for loading in the Organ (see the Model loading and initialisation class diagram below), but also calculating the properties of the loaded Organ that the other classes rely on. In order to provide other classes ease of access to these properties, ModelHandler is made a singleton. Although the Singleton pattern is often seen as an antipattern, its use here is appropriate as only one model is ever loaded into the application at any given time. Our EventManager class (see below) is also a singleton.

Publisher-Subscriber pattern

To reduce the dependencies between classes in our codebase we utilised the Publisher-Subscriber pattern. To do this, we created the EventManager class. The EventManager holds a reference to a number of different events, each of which is designed to trigger a certain action within the application. Each event has a corresponding public method which can be called to invoke the event. As the class is a singleton, any class can trigger these events without a reference to the EventManager, and any class can subscribe to these events without a reference to the EventManager. So for instance, a button can fire an event when clicked. Any scripts that relate to this button will subscribe to this event. However, these scripts do not break if this button is deleted, nor does the button break if the scripts are deleted. Neither party requires a reference to one another as all communication is handled by the EventManager. Any class that listens for events implements the IEventManagerListener interface.

Factory pattern

Our application is shipped with six example glb models that can be viewed by the user. These can be selected from a dropdown menu. Each example model is represented by a corresponding class that is a subclass of the abstract Organ class. We use the OrganFactory class to instantiate the appropriate subclass of Organ based on the model chosen to view by the user at runtime. If the user chooses to load a model from local storage, the default concrete subclass of the abstract Organ class is instantiated.

Implementation

Rendering and Initialsing the Models

Our application loads and renders GLB AND GLTF files at runtime. Unity, however, does not natively support these file types. In order to load the models into the application we made use of GLTFUtility, an open source library that supports runtime import of GLB/GLTF files into Unity. It converts the data in the GLB/GLTF files into a number of Unity GameObjects with a MeshFilter ,MeshRenderer and Material component attached. Each of these GameObjects represents a segment of the loaded model.

  • The MeshFilter and MeshRenderer components determine how the geometry/shape of the loaded model are renderered.
  • The Materials component determines how the surface of the model is rendered (the colour of the model, how it interacts with Unity lights and how much of the geometry should be drawn to the screen).

Organ.cs

ModelHandler.cs

The model is loaded and initialised within a coroutine. The condition in the yield statement is evaluated each frame, and if it is false, control is passed back to the calling function so that other code can continue to execute while the model is being loaded. Once the condition is true, the remaining code executes and fully initialises the model. The line MaterialAssigner.assignToAllChildren is described more in later sections. Note that the ModelHandler class is a singleton. Although singletons are often seen as an antipattern, its use here is appopriate since there is only ever one model being renderered in the scene. The size and position of the model do directly influence a number of other classes, so having ModelHandler as singleton prevents them from needing to hold a reference a ModelHandler object.

Camera Controls

The user is able to rotate the camera about a pivot position, zoom in and out (using the ScrollWheel, onscreen buttons and the w and s keys), and translate the camera left, right, up and down using the y, g, h and b keys. This pivot is initialised to the centre of the model (the modelCentre variable of the ModelHandler class).

CameraController.cs

The rotation of the camera is determined by the direction vector of the pointer. This rotation is achieved using the Rotate method, which is a public method of the Transform class provided in the UnityEngine namespace. All Unity GameObjects have a Transform component attached that describes its position and orientation in the scene. The displacement (in the z axis) of the camera from the pivot is determined by how much the user has zoomed in/out on the view of the model. Every frame, the camera's position is set equal to the position of the pivot. It is then rotated using the rotate method, and translated away from the pivot using the Translate method. This creates the desired effect, in that the camera is always centered on the pivot.

The displacement vector is also used to store the amount displaced in the x and y axes when the keybinds are used to pan the camera

CameraController.cs

In order to allow models of different sizes to be loaded into the application and viewed in the same way, the initial displacement of the camera from the pivot is determined by modelRadius: the radius of the model (more accurately, the radius of the sphere that bounds the renderer of the model). This radius is also used to determine the scrollspeed attribute of the CameraController class, which means the speed at which the user can zoom in is proportional to the size of the model.

CameraResetter.cs

The position of the camera can also be reset by the user. This is carried out by means of the Lerp function, which performs a Linear Interpolation between two values. For the lerp called on the camera's position, the value returned on any given frame is equivalent to startPos + (TargetPos - startPos) * ratio, where ratio is the ratio of the elapsed time since the camera started resetting to the desired total reset time, which is set to 2 seconds in our application. When the ratio is one, the Camera's position will be equal to the target position and the lerp function will no longer be called.

Finally, the user can adjust the position about which the camera is centred using the pivot controller. The PivotController class holds a reference to the pivot itself (which is represented in the scene as a sphere), as well as a reference to three sliders. These sliders can be used to control the position of the pivot in the x,y and z axes. In much the same way that the scrollspeed and cameraDistance variables in the CameraController class are proportional to the modelRadius, the maximum and minimum values of these sliders is determined by modelRadius.

Changing Model Colour and Opacity

To allow the opacity and colour of the segments of loaded models to be changed, our code directly accesses the renderers of the models.

MaterialAssigner.cs

GetComponent<Renderer>() returns the renderer of the GameObject/model segment passed to the functions. Note:GetComponent<> is a very expensive operation, which is why we obtain a reference to the renderer once at the start of the function to prevent the need to call it again. By changing the values of its material, we change how the Unity renderer displays the GameObjects on screen. MaterialAssigner is a static class and houses all methods related to the assignment and manipulation of the materials of loaded models. The functions above in particular are called as part of callback actions to different events triggered by the user (see below).

ModelHandler.cs

We initially encountered some draw ordering issues when attempting to adjust the opacity of the different segments of the model. We resolved these issues by making changes to the shaders our models were using (see Cross Sectional Viewer) and by adjusting the RenderQueue attribute of the material applied to each of the segments of the model. The higher the RenderQueue value, the later an object is drawn to the screen. By making it so that segments that are hidden by other segments are drawn to the screen later, it is possible to see them beneath the outer segments when the opacity of the outer segments are reduced. This is the purpose of the MaterialAssigner.assignToAllChildren function called in the loadModel coroutine above.

Creating, Storing and Viewing Annotations

Annotations are used for storing text about a particular model in a particular view of that model. To clarify, if the user creates an annotation, the current state of the scene, along with the text they entered, is saved. Selecting this annotation will return the user to that exact view of the model, and display the text in a textbox. This data is stored in the form of an AnnotationData object.

AnnotationData.cs

When a user confirms they want to create an annotation, a new AnnotationData object is instantiated and its fields populated with the relevant data. It is then converted into a JSON string and written to a new file whose name is the same as the title entered by the user. This file is stored in a directory whose name is the same as the model currently being viewed. This directory is initialised as soon as the model is loaded into the program for the first time, in the same directory that the application runs in.

Annotation.cs

The user can select an annotation to view from a dropdown menu. When the user clicks the button to view the dropdown menu, the relevant folder is searched for JSON files. All these files are parsed from JSON back into AnnotationData objects, which are stored in a list.

AnnotationSelector.cs

When an annotation from the dropdown list is selected, an OnSelectAnnotation event is fired which holds the relevant AnnotationData object. All subscribers of this event will restore the part of the scene that they are responsible for back to the state encapsulated by the AnnotationData object received (for instance, CameraController restores camera position,rotation and displacement, ModelHandler restores the colours of the model segments, etc).

Cross Sectional Viewer

To implement this feature we made use of surface shaders written in High Level Shader Language. Surface shaders provide a code generation approach to writing shaders that interact with light in Unity. The concept that our cross-sectional viewer shader employs is similar to the idea of occlusion culling, in which an object will only be rendered by a camera if it is between the near and far clipping planes of the camera. If an object is partially between these two planes, only its polygons between the two planes will be drawn to the screen. Our shader (which we based heavily on this open-source shader, as well as the shaders included in the GLTFUtility library that were already being used) determines which pixels of the model should not be drawn to the screen, based on the position and orientation of a plane that can be controlled by the user using sliders.
If the dot product of:

  • The distance between the world space position of the pixel on the model currently being evaluated by the shader and the centre of the plane AND
  • The plane's normal
    • is positive, then the current pixel is above the plane, so is discarded.

    Testing

    PLAYMODE TESTS IN UNITY

    The nature of our application made it fundamentally difficult to Unit Test. As our application is built in Unity, the majority of our classes inherit from the MonoBehaviour class. Unlike other classes in C#, MonoBehaviours cannot be instantiated directly, but must be instead attached to an existing Unity GameObject using the AddComponent<> function. Furthermore, due to a number of classes being dependent on the loaded model, testing individual modules of code proved difficult. 

    However, using the Unity Testing FrameWork, we were able to create tests to verify a few of the core functionalities of our system. Namely, we wrote tests to verify that the Events of our EventManager class could be invoked and received by the relevant listener classes, whether the models were being loaded with the expected number of segments and that our AnnotationData objects could be parsed to and from JSON.

    USER ACCEPTANCE TESTING

    ALPHA TESTING

    Over the course of the second term of our development, we regularly sent builds of our project to our clients. Our clients would provide us with feedback on the project with each iteration we sent, often making suggestions on how things could be changed. Although this testing was relatively informal, it was hugely beneficial in that:

The following requirements from our MoSCoW list were actually gathered from our clients testing and providing feedback on iterations of the application:

      • Have tool-tips appear over elements of the GUI when the pointer hovers over them.
      • Allow the user to change the position about which the camera is centred.
      • Have a full-screen mode of the application, which hides all GUI elements.
      • Allow the user to change the brightness of the background.

    • In addition to testing usability, this process served as a form of compatibility testing. Sending these builds allowed us to verify that our application would run correctly on Windows devices beyond the devices we were using for development. Specifically, we were able to verify that our GUI scaled well for different screen sizes.

    Critical Evaluation

    UI

    Simplicity and clarity are the prior principles for our UI design. As the whole user interface is designed in the style of Neumorphism, we believe our UI meets our goals. All images in our application (except for the clients' logos) are designed by using Adobe Photoshop. In order to improve the user experience, we sent our application to our clients, friends and families, and received a lot of positive feedback from them. After constant modification and attempt, our UI becomes better. To give an example, we modified some icons to make their appearance and function do match. And since our users have a wide range of ages, this simple and intuitive design will make it easier for older users to use, while still being fashionable. As a result, we have a lot of confidence in our UI.

    Stability

    In the progress of executing our application and testing, no phenomenon of crash has been noticed. Al, the bugs have been fixed while programming. However, some warnings may cause the application run slowly. This can be improved during future development.

    Functionality

    We have implemented 100% of the requirements on our MoSCoW list. Over the course of development, we've frequently sent builds of our project to our clients and they have been happy with what we've accomplished. The small amount of beta testing we carried out has also been very encouraging in that potential users of our application have enjoyed the experience of using it.

    Compatibility

    The application should run on any machine running Windows 10. We know that it runs well on the machines our clients use, as well as the three main machines that we used throughout development.

    Efficiency

    Apart from the example models, we have tested loading other models into the application using the open source models hosted on the Github of KHRONOS GROUP, the creators of GLTF and GLB files. All the models can be loaded in and successfully rendered in 2 seconds. However, as all these models are not medical models and are relatively simple to construct, we do not know how long it will take while loading in and render a professional medical model, which is more complex. This can be tested in future work.

    Maintainability

    To ensure that our codebase is maintainable, we have commented every class we have written. All assets of our Unity project are also logically separated into folders to make development as straightforward as possible. However, the code design of the system as a whole could definitely be improved upon.

    Project Management

    Over the course of the project, we scheduled weekly meetings with our clients and would aim to demonstrate a new feature/improvement to an existing feature at each meeting. This helped us feel less overwhelmed by how huge the project we were undertaking felt, and ultimately helped us manage our time better.
    As our project is built in Unity, we were able to make use of the services Unity Collaborate and Unity Cloud Build. This meant that whenever we pushed any new code to the cloud, a new build of our project would be created. This helped save time in creating new builds manually and made our workflow much smoother.
    However, one area in which our project management could've been improved was how much we communicated outside of our client meetings. We did communicate over MS teams, but it was sometimes difficult to get a hold of one another and this led to long periods of time when no communication was made. Furthermore, our project became very difficult to manage when Shuai lost internet connection for three weeks and the team consisted of just Bryn. After this happened it was very difficult to re-establish a robust project plan and did make the few weeks of development extremely difficult./p>

    Future Work

    • Our application could be fully integrated into the existing HoloRepository ecosystem. In an ideal world, a clinician would be able to select a patient scan, which would then be immediately converted into a GLB model and loaded and rendered by our application. Using our DICOM viewer feature, the original patient scans could then be viewed along-side the newly generated 3D model.
    • Secondly, higher fidelity rendering of the models could be carried out, which could involve more detailed texturing and raytracing. This is something that is already being looked into by a third year student.
    • Thirdly, the application could be made cross platform, so that clinicians and educators could use it on other Operating systems, as well as their phones and tablets.
    • Finally, the ability to open the application from well formatted URL links. The parameters of these links could include the model file to be loaded, along with the attributes of the AnnotationData class that we are currently using to store a given view of the model. Clicking on one of these links would render the model passed as the parameter, and immediately take the user to the view of the model determined by the other parameters.
